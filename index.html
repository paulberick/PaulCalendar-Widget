Dedicated To Persephone

Beautiful Persephone once danced in sunlit meadows, daughter of Demeter, goddess of the harvest. Her laughter wove through wildflowers, her steps coaxing blooms from the earth. She was spring incarnate, her world a tapestry of eternal growth under her mother’s golden gaze. Beneath this splendor, however, the earth held shadows. Hades, lord of the underworld, beheld her radiance and yearned to claim it. In a moment, the ground split, and he swept her into the depths, her cries echoing as petals fell.

Time passed in the underworld, Persephone transformed. No longer just a maiden, she slowly became Hades’ queen. Her heart ached for her former life, but grew to admire the dark enchantment of the underworld. The earth withered and froze as Demeter wept, inconsolable, until Zeus intervened with a divine pact: Persephone would spend half of each year below, ruling with Hades, and half above, reunited with Demeter and returning lush life to nature. Thus began the cycle — when she ascends, spring awakens, flowers unfurl, and the hemisphere sings. When she descends autumn fades to winter and the earth rests in her absence.

Persephone’s rhythm guides this planner as part of us joins her journey between these worlds of lights and shadows.

This Calendar

Inspired by the heavens and Persephone’s journey, this timepiece follows nature’s pulse, breaking free from the Gregorian calendar. This calendar demonstrates a living rhythm shaped by the sun, moon, and earth. Our year begins with the spring equinox — her return — as Day 1 and unfolds in seven-day weeks, and four week Quatrains, of which there are exactly thirteen, plus one final day of year-end celebration. The moon’s cycles, called Moonths, begin with each full moon and guide our evenings under her gentle glow. Together, these solar and lunar rhythms offer a balanced, intuitive way to organize your life. Let the sun shape your days, the moon govern your nights, in harmony.

What Has Changed

The year commences with the spring equinox. For those who dwell far from Earth’s ecliptic, seasons remain a perpetual rhythm, and spring — when nature awakens from dormancy in vibrant fashion — marks the truest season for new beginnings. Thus, we align our celebrations with the Earth’s reawakening.

The year is divided into thirteen months. The term “month” derives not from solar cycles but from “moonth,” reflecting the lunar rhythm of approximately 29.5 days (with a variance of thirteen hours). Dividing the year into thirteen 28-day months better approximates the moon’s cadence and harmonizes with the solar year, totaling 364 days. The remaining day — or two in a leap year — becomes a joyous year-end celebration, unbound by the weekly cycle. This structure means that once annually, a Solday (Sunday) will not follow a Paxday (Saturday) but instead be separated by one or two Festdays; a minor trade-off for months of equal length, each beginning and ending on the same weekday, and a calendar more closely attuned to lunar cycles.

The weekdays are renamed to honor the sources of our light, life, and natural rhythms, as well as the human condition woven within them. The Gregorian calendar’s weekdays draw from disparate mythologies—Sun, Moon, Tiw (war), Odin (death), Thor (thunder), Freya (love), and Saturn. In contrast, this calendar embraces the elegant narrative of Persephone, whose story mirrors the cycles of both nature and humanity. The days, inspired by celestial and elemental forces, are: Solday, for the sun’s radiant life; Lunaday, for the moon’s gentle guidance; Terraday, for the Earth’s steadfast embrace; Waterday, for water, embodying Persephone’s transformative essence, raw emotion, and power; Dreamday, for the underworld and dreamworld we enter nightly; Fireday, representing Hades, the destructive yet renewing force of change, as well as the poignant beauty of loss as we shed past selves; and finally Paxday, for peace, found not in numbness, but in embracing discomfort, uncertainty, and fear as part of our dualistic human experience.

Throughout history, the names of months and moons have varied across cultures, often reflecting seasonal labors or natural phenomena. Full moons, with their luminous glow, have long facilitated nighttime travel and celebration. In this calendar, the thirteen 28-day “moonths” are numbered simply and named for the full moon each contains, drawing from the rich tapestry of lunar names across time. These names are:

  • Renewal – Marking the year’s rebirth.
  • Flower – For the vibrant blooms of spring.
  • Dance – Evoking the lively gatherings of this season.
  • Water – Honoring the life-giving element.
  • Hot – Reflecting the warmth of summer’s peak.
  • Pilgrim – For the journeys undertaken in this season.
  • Love – Inspired by the tender hues of summer sunsets.
  • Gather – For the harvest in all its forms.
  • Ice – For the chill that descends.
  • Spirit – For the introspection of darkening days.
  • Bone – Honoring death’s stark beauty.
  • Crow – For the playful, wild reminders of mortality.
  • Ancestor – A name of permanence for the rare thirteenth moon.

I hope this calendar supports your health, growth, and brings you joy for many moons.
